Tools to Achieve Assessment and Evaluation

The most common method we think of when we think of assessing students is traditional classroom testing, like pre and post- tests and exams. However, there are many tools to complete assessment available at our disposal. Let’s review all of the tools at our disposal.

 

Traditional Classroom Testing

The above four evaluation types are generally conducted by paper and pencil tests or computer generated tests. The collection of quantitative data can provide a lot of information. We use tests be they formal or informal, to measure an outcome and make sense of that data. This might be  a standardized test, or a teacher made test. For example a fifty item, multiple choice history exam. There are a variety of types of tests including; achievement tests, personality tests, aptitude tests, and ability or intelligence tests. During this class, we will be focusing in on how to develop achievement tests.

 

Student Achievement Tests

Achievement tests assess an individual's level of knowledge in a particular domain, like the history test I mentioned above. Achievement tests play an important role in the learning environment by determining what a student has learned and comparing it to what other students know. There are a variety of types of achievement test. Achievement tests are the most common test given. These tests can be teacher made, or standardized. Teacher made tests are constructed by a teacher and the effort made in establishing the validity and reliability are often non-existent or lacking in their thoroughness. There's nothing at all wrong with teacher made test they are just very situation specific and designed to suit a particular teacher’s needs. For the majority of this class, we will be discussing the creation of teacher-made tests.  We will discuss standardized tests later in the class.

Performance Based Assessment

While we can use traditional testing there are also other ways of assessing student performance toward meeting the objectives in a course. We can use various types of performance-based assessments. The most common examples of these is:

·        Observation

·        Anecdotal Records

·        Rating Scales

·        Checklists

·        Rubrics

·        Self-Assessment

·        Peer Assessment

However, the most effective means of obtaining data is observation . Observation is the purpose of collecting evaluative information by performance based data, for example; reviewing anecdotal records, rating scales, and checklists, peer appraisals, and self-reporting methods. In this section, we will review the techniques used by instructors: anecdotal records, rating scales, and checklists. We will cover self and peer assessments and rubrics in coming weeks.


Anecdotal records  are factual descriptions of meaningful incidents and events that the instructor has observed. The instructional objectives will guide the selection of events and incidents to record. This is generally useful for disciplinary incidents, violations, and violations of safety rules. These selections depict actual behavior in a natural setting. The major problem with these types of evaluations is their lack of objectivity. It is important to record an incident as soon as possible. Keep facts and inferences separate. You should try to record both positive and negative behavioral incidents to show growth as well as problems. Single incidents rarely indicate a pattern of behavior, so it is better to observe the person for more than one incident before making any inferences about his or her behavior.

The use of rating scales  provides systematic procedures for reporting the observer’s judgments. Usually, the rating scale consists of a set of characteristics or qualities to be judged and some type of scale is made in conjunction with the instructional objectives so that instructors can observe specific behaviors. The rating scale will also provide a common frame of reference for comparing all students on the same set of characteristics. This common frame provides a convenient method for recording observer’s judgments. Rating scales are useful in evaluating learning outcomes associated with procedures, products, or personal/social development. However, you should be aware that rating scales have a tendency to inflate scores. Conversely, it is conceivable that you would rate everyone severely low. Another common occurrence is to rate everyone average or what is called central tendency error. Subjectivity on the part of the evaluator may affect the entire rating as high or low depending on the general impression of the evaluator towards the entire evaluation process. There are several points of consideration regarding the effective use of rating scales. The items being observed are directly related to the instructional objectives. Therefore, if objectives are written properly, it becomes easy to identify performances using the rating scale. The rating scale must confine items to those directly observable. The characteristics should be clearly defined. When you evaluate using a rating scale, you should omit characteristics you feel you are not qualified to judge. If possible, ratings from several evaluators should be combined to arrive at a common rating.

Checklists  are similar to rating scales, but they just call for a yes or no rather than a degree of frequency of the item selected. Most checklists focus on procedures and processes rather than the end result. Evaluating performance skills that can be divided into a series of specific actions can be accomplished using a checklist.
You may find that by involving your students in developing and using rating scales and checklists, and holding follow-up individual conferences, the students better understand instructional objectives, recognize their progress toward the objectives, and diagnose their own strengths and weaknesses. This process helps students learn how to diagnose their own performance.

Rubrics are performance assessment that used to determine if a student is able to show their learning. In this way, it is an assessment by the teacher of the students “authentic work”. (Taylor, 2008). These include a variety of activities including products and live performances.  Products might include things like; 1) written reports, reflections, mathematical models, websites, paintings. Live performances might include things like; 1) speeches, presentations, musical performance, athletic performance, scientific investigations, debates. These performance assessments include specific rules for scoring (evaluating) the performance and or the product of the performance.  These rules can be set up into a rubric. This rubric should be a systematic scoring of the rules.
